Saving Our City One Plastic Bag at a Time

Editorial — Gulftowne Gazette

1The editorial staff at the Gulftowne Gazette says three cheers for the Gulftowne Ordinance Committee! Finally, they are serious about making reusable shopping bags mandatory in every store with Proposition 328.

2Americans concerned about our environment agree that plastic bags litter our landscape and threaten wildlife. Many cities across the country and around the globe have banned their use. Paper bags are not the ideal replacement either. Cutting down trees to make paper greatly diminishes our forests. The obvious solution is to mandate that consumers bring reusable bags when they go shopping.

3Reusable bags are made from canvas or other cloth, man-made fibers, or sturdy plastic. These bags are lightweight, washable, and durable — many styles can last for years. A widely reported study found that the United States uses 100 billion disposable plastic shopping bags every year!

4The cost to consumers is minimal — some reusable bags cost less than a dollar. Many retailers already charge fees for disposable bags. Eventually, purchasing reusable bags is more cost-effective.

5Those opposing mandatory use cite health concerns based on a study showing bacteria growth in reusable bags. This issue can easily be addressed by washing the bags frequently and using separate bags for meats and produce.

Letter to the Editor — Against Proposition 328

Theo Jones, Owner, Gulftowne Gifts

1I strongly disagree with your editorial on the new reusable bag ordinance. Shop owners in Gulftowne rely on tourists to make a living. Unfortunately, the Ordinance Committee did not think beyond the grocery store when considering this terrible ordinance.

2I own a gift shop. Tourists buy mementos, figurines, t-shirts, postcards, and beach supplies. Many vacation purchases fit into bags no larger than an envelope. Requiring our visitors to bring reusable bags for such purchases makes absolutely no sense.

3Most shop owners cannot afford to provide reusable bags to customers. The bags used now have logos, addresses, phone numbers, and website addresses. They are not free. Where in the ordinance is the cost to make new multi-sized bags with store information?

4Shop owners are also concerned about the environment, but we don’t want tourists shopping in the next town where paper and plastic bags are still used. This requirement may make sense in a grocery store, but penalizing other types of shops who don’t really contribute to the problem is unacceptable.
