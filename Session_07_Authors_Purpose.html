Passage A — Informational

1The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since 2009 — the longest period without an increase in the law’s history. Adjusted for inflation, today’s minimum wage is worth less in real purchasing power than it was in the 1960s.

2Currently, more than 1.1 million workers in the United States earn at or below the federal minimum wage. Many of these workers are adults supporting families, not teenagers in entry-level jobs as is commonly assumed.

3States have the authority to set their own minimum wages above the federal level, and as of 2024, more than 30 states had done so.

Passage B — Persuasive

1The federal minimum wage is a moral failure and an economic lie. When a worker earns $7.25 an hour — $290 for a 40-hour week, before taxes — they cannot afford rent, groceries, or health care in any city in America.

2We have allowed corporations to profit from poverty wages while taxpayers foot the bill through public assistance programs that low-wage workers depend on to survive. It is long past time for Congress to act.

3Raising the minimum wage to $15 an hour is not radical — it is the bare minimum of human decency.

Passage C — Explanatory

1Minimum wage laws set a legal floor on what employers can pay workers. In the United States, the federal minimum wage applies to most private and public sector employees.

2To change the federal minimum wage, Congress must pass legislation and the president must sign it. States and localities can set their own minimums above the federal level — and many have — but they cannot go below it.

3Some economists argue that minimum wage increases reduce employment; others argue the effect is minimal or positive. The debate continues among researchers and policymakers.

Questions
Question 1
What is the author’s purpose in Passage A?
  • ATo argue that the minimum wage should be increased immediately.
  • BTo inform readers about the current state of the federal minimum wage.
  • CTo explain how Congress changes federal wage laws.
  • DTo persuade workers to advocate for their rights.
Correct: B — Passage A presents facts without advocating for a position. Its purpose is to inform — not argue.
Question 2
Which word from Passage B most clearly reveals the author’s persuasive purpose?
  • Afederal
  • Bfailure
  • CCongress
  • Dworkers
Correct: B — ‘Failure’ is a value judgment, not a neutral fact. It signals the author is evaluating and arguing, not simply reporting.
Question 3
How is Passage C’s purpose DIFFERENT from Passage B’s, even though both cover the same topic?
  • APassage C supports raising the minimum wage; Passage B opposes it.
  • BPassage C explains how the wage system works without taking a position; Passage B argues for a specific change.
  • CPassage C is written for economists; Passage B is written for workers.
  • DPassage C uses more emotional language than Passage B.
Correct: B — Passage C is explanatory — it describes the wage system. Passage B is persuasive — it argues for a specific change.
Question 4
Which technique does the author of Passage B use to make their argument feel urgent?
  • ACiting statistics from multiple research studies.
  • BQuoting workers who earn minimum wage.
  • CUsing loaded language like ‘moral failure,’ ‘poverty wages,’ and ‘bare minimum of human decency.’
  • DPresenting both sides of the debate fairly.
Correct: C — Loaded language creates emotional impact rather than relying on evidence alone — a key persuasive technique.
★ GED TEST TIP: Author’s purpose is revealed by tone and word choice. Loaded language signals persuasion; neutral reporting signals information or explanation.
