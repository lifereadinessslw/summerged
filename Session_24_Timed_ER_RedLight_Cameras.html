Speech: Proposal for Automatic Traffic Light Cameras

Councilman Lorenzo Hart — Baler Town Council

1The cuts to this year’s budget mean that fewer funds are available for many town departments. I propose installing automatic traffic light cameras at 10 major intersections in town. Automating this process will allow our police officers to focus on duties that require human attention, saving the cost of hiring additional officers.

2Automatic traffic cameras have well-documented benefits. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration states that running red lights causes hundreds of traffic fatalities and thousands of injuries annually. According to a 2010 report by the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety, red-light cameras have lowered these numbers in hundreds of U.S. cities. A 2005 Federal Highway Administration study found a 25% decrease in front-into-side auto accidents in seven cities that use red-light cameras.

3Currently, drivers who get away with red-light violations tend to repeat the behavior. A 2009 study of a program in Montgomery County, Maryland, showed an average 78% decline in the number of red-light tickets issued after the cameras had operated for a year.

4In addition, by citing violators who may have gone unpunished without cameras, our town would increase revenue through additional traffic fines. A single intersection in Lawrence Township, New Jersey, generated over $1 million in fines in only one year. Please support this proposal.

Letter to the Editor: Automatic Cameras Not an Automatic Solution

Madison Caan, civil engineer — Baler Herald

1The town council’s proposal to install 10 automatic red-light cameras is a proposal to waste money. The company AutoCamera Inc. has promised to waive installation fees that would normally run $50,000–$100,000 per intersection. However, that same company will charge us $5,000–$6,000 each month per camera. That is at least $60,000 per year for one camera — installing 10 will cost at least $600,000 per year, enough to pay the salaries of new officers.

2These cameras create other unexpected costs. In New Jersey and New York, several cities have been sued over the timing of traffic lights with automatic cameras. In a single December in New Jersey, traffic light company ATS settled 16 separate class-action lawsuits. Any new fines collected may pale in comparison to increased legal fees.

3The safety benefits are also unclear. A 2005 National Highway Traffic Safety Administration report says that some cities using cameras have seen a decrease in front-into-side accidents, but rear-end collisions have increased. Speeding drivers notice cameras at the last minute and brake abruptly, causing accidents behind them.

4In every way, actual traffic police officers are superior to automatic red-light cameras. The only investment we need to make is hiring more officers.
