Harry S. Truman’s 1947 NAACP Address

1On June 29, 1947, President Harry S. Truman addressed the 38th Annual Conference of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP). Opening with a determined tone, Truman urged Americans to work together to repair racial divisions. He suggested that the country had reached a turning point — that for the first time in its history, America was ready to guarantee freedom and equality to all its citizens.

2He continued with an assertion that the government should protect all its peoples: all Americans should possess decent homes, adequate medical care, worthwhile employment, and the right to a fair trial.

3Truman did not paint a one-sided picture, however. At the heart of his speech was a sobering account of the social challenges facing post-World War II America, including racially motivated intimidation and mob violence. He did not dwell on these grim topics but cited them as fuel for promoting change.

4After observing the complexities of expanding federal laws, Truman pointed to his administration’s efforts, including the 1946 appointment of the President’s Committee on Civil Rights and a request that Congress extend civil rights protections to residents of Guam and American Samoa. He also cited Eleanor Roosevelt’s work chairing the United Nations Commission on Human Rights.

5Truman concluded by invoking words Abraham Lincoln had written in 1862, calling on the nation to remain united — not only for itself, but for future generations.

Questions
Question 1
Truman cites Eleanor Roosevelt’s UN work. Which rhetorical technique is this?
  • AAd hominem — attacking an opponent’s character.
  • BFalse cause — claiming one event caused another.
  • CAppeal to authority — using a respected figure to strengthen credibility.
  • DEither/or — presenting only two possible outcomes.
Correct: C — Citing Roosevelt’s international work borrows the authority of a respected figure to reinforce Truman’s own commitment to human rights.
Question 2
The word ‘however’ in paragraph 3 signals a shift. What does the paragraph after ‘however’ do in this argument?
  • AIt contradicts the main point of the speech.
  • BIt introduces problems that Truman uses as evidence for why change is needed.
  • CIt presents the opposing side’s full argument.
  • DIt summarizes what Truman had already said.
Correct: B — After ‘however,’ Truman describes racial violence — not to undermine hope, but to justify the urgency of action.
Question 3
Which of the following would be a logical fallacy if used to argue AGAINST Truman’s civil rights position?
  • ACiting federal statistics on racial violence in post-war America.
  • BArguing that Truman’s proposals would be too costly based on a budget analysis.
  • C‘If we guarantee rights to one group, we’ll have to guarantee everything to everyone — and society will collapse.’
  • DPointing out that civil rights protections were already working in some states.
Correct: C — This is a slippery slope fallacy — assuming an extreme chain of consequences without evidence.
Question 4
Truman invokes Lincoln’s 1862 words at the end. What makes this a rhetorical technique rather than just a historical reference?
  • ALincoln wrote about civil rights, so quoting him proves Truman is right.
  • BIt aligns Truman’s cause with a universally respected figure and appeals to shared national heritage.
  • CIt suggests that Truman’s policies are identical to Lincoln’s.
  • DIt proves that the civil rights movement has been ongoing since 1862.
Correct: B — Invoking Lincoln is a deliberate rhetorical choice — it borrows moral authority and frames Truman’s cause as part of a long American tradition.
★ GED TEST TIP: Logical fallacies feel convincing — that is what makes them dangerous. Ask: does the argument actually PROVE the conclusion, or does it skip a step, attack a person, or use extreme scenarios instead of evidence?
