Eleanor Roosevelt and the Declaration of Human Rights

1In 1948, during Eleanor Roosevelt’s tenure as chairman of the United Nations Commission on Human Rights, she gave a speech, “The Struggle for Human Rights,” at the Sorbonne in Paris, France.

2She began by informing her audience that her purpose was to talk with them about the preservation of human freedom. She tackled several topics, including the commission’s Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the difficulties the members of the United Nations faced during the drafting process.

3Roosevelt outlined the two components of the International Bill of Rights. The first part served as a declaration of the basic human rights that any individual is entitled to, no matter where he or she lives. The second part was a covenant on human rights to be presented to every nation. Once ratified, the bill would be used to reshape any national laws that did not conform to its principles.

4At the time of Roosevelt’s speech, four nations — including the Soviet Union — had abstained from accepting the bill. Drawing her audience’s attention to these nations, Roosevelt described the difficulties in deciding upon universal definitions of democracy. She compared the United States and the Soviet Union, noting that the US government did not punish any political viewpoints expressed in its newspapers, while she suggested the USSR would close papers that criticized its political philosophy.

5She concluded with a call to unity, passionately encouraging delegates to overwhelmingly approve the Declaration of Human Rights.

Questions
Question 1
What was Eleanor Roosevelt’s PRIMARY purpose in this speech?
  • ATo criticize the Soviet Union’s record on human rights.
  • BTo encourage nations to approve the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.
  • CTo explain the history of the United Nations Commission.
  • DTo describe the differences between democracy and communism.
Correct: B — The closing call to action — urging approval of the Declaration — reveals her primary purpose.
Question 2
Roosevelt mentions the USSR would close critical newspapers. Is this evidence or an assertion?
  • AEvidence — she cites a specific case where the USSR closed a newspaper.
  • BEvidence — it comes from a United Nations report.
  • CAssertion — she suggests this is true but provides no verifiable source.
  • DAssertion — she is repeating what other delegates told her.
Correct: C — ‘She suggested’ signals this is her claim, not verified fact. On the GED, unverified assertions are weaker than specific, sourced evidence.
Question 3
How does Roosevelt address the nations that abstained from the bill?
  • AShe asks the UN to remove them from the commission.
  • BShe acknowledges that defining democracy universally is genuinely difficult.
  • CShe warns those nations of consequences.
  • DShe ignores their abstentions.
Correct: B — Roosevelt uses the abstentions to discuss the complexity of defining democracy — a credibility-building move.
Question 4
What text structure best describes how the passage is organized?
  • AProblem and solution — a problem is introduced and a fix is offered.
  • BCompare and contrast — two positions are placed side by side throughout.
  • CChronological — the speech is summarized in the order it was delivered.
  • DCause and effect — each decision leads to a consequence.
Correct: C — The passage follows the speech from opening to conclusion, making chronological the correct structure.
★ GED TEST TIP: Ask three questions about every piece of evidence: Is it relevant? Is it sufficient? Is it credible? An assertion with no source is weaker than cited, verifiable data.
