Diamond Mining: Harmful to the Environment

by Tavin McLeavitt

1The diamond mining industry directly affects an estimated 10 million people around the world. While some companies and countries have made a fortune from diamond mining, the industry has a long history of abusing human rights and exploiting workers. The industry’s devastating impact on the environment is another reason why diamond mining should be banned.

2During any mining process, the soil, water, and air surrounding a mine become polluted. Large quantities of soil must be removed, reducing available natural land resources. Any mine’s expansion encroaches on natural animal habitats, decreasing wildlife populations.

3Diamond mining also affects water supply and quality. Water is used to extract diamonds, but water is a scarce resource in Africa, where many of the world’s diamond mines are located. Canada’s Northwest Territories provides an example: companies have drained twenty lakes, causing massive changes to the environment and disrupting the natural habitats of many species.

4Finally, energy sources used to operate diamond mines create greenhouse gases. Diesel fuels, electricity, and hydrocarbons used in diamond mining all release harmful carbons into the air. The long-term environmental impact is simply not worth the financial gains a few corporations and countries desire.

Diamond Mining Companies Find New Ways to Preserve the Environment

by Shaunna Rangel

1The diamond mining industry is not the environmental villain it is often portrayed to be. Although any mining operation can disturb delicate ecology, diamond mining companies have proven to be responsive to public concern and have worked hard to reduce the impact of their operations.

2A number of wildlife preserves have been established to protect and breed species of animals endangered by diamond mines. The Diavik Diamond Mine in Canada has implemented an advisory board to study ways to protect fish near the mining area — a program so successful it expanded to include arctic deer. In South Africa, the national parks system worked with the Peace Parks Foundation and the diamond industry to set up a conservation area near the Venetia Diamond Mine.

3Governments are trying to reduce waste and reuse resources while mining for diamonds. Environmental management companies closely monitor the mines and routinely publish reports. Many mines do more than the minimum required by regulated standards. Researchers are working on new, less water-intensive methods of diamond extraction.

4With support and regulation from governments, the mining industry can safely continue to benefit a large number of people who live in the countries where diamonds are mined.
