Harry S. Truman’s 1947 NAACP Address

1On June 29, 1947, President Harry S. Truman addressed the 38th Annual Conference of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP). Opening with a determined tone, Truman urged Americans to work together to repair racial divisions. He suggested that the country had reached a turning point — that for the first time in its history, America was ready to guarantee freedom and equality to all its citizens.

2He continued with an assertion that the government should protect all its peoples: all Americans should possess decent homes, adequate medical care, worthwhile employment, and the right to a fair trial.

3Truman did not paint a one-sided picture, however. At the heart of his speech was a sobering account of the social challenges facing post-World War II America, including racially motivated intimidation and mob violence. He did not dwell on these grim topics but cited them as fuel for promoting change.

4After observing the complexities of expanding federal laws, Truman pointed to his administration’s efforts, including the 1946 appointment of the President’s Committee on Civil Rights and a request that Congress extend civil rights protections to residents of Guam and American Samoa. He also cited Eleanor Roosevelt’s work chairing the United Nations Commission on Human Rights.

5Truman concluded by invoking words Abraham Lincoln had written in 1862, calling on the nation to remain united — not only for itself, but for future generations.

Questions
Question 1
Why does Truman choose to speak at an NAACP conference? What does this choice reveal about audience and purpose?
  • AHe was legally required to address civil rights organizations before Congress.
  • BSpeaking directly to those most affected signals genuine commitment — not just political posturing.
  • CHe believed the NAACP had more political power than Congress at the time.
  • DHe was unable to get time on the Congressional schedule.
Correct: B — Audience choice is a rhetorical decision. Speaking to the NAACP directly signals real commitment and builds trust with those most affected.
Question 2
Truman mentions ‘racially motivated intimidation and mob violence.’ What does including this historical context serve?
  • AIt shows that Truman believes the situation is hopeless.
  • BIt contradicts his overall message of optimism.
  • CIt provides evidence that justifies the urgency of his civil rights proposals.
  • DIt is an emotional appeal that weakens his argument.
Correct: C — Truman names the real problem — violence and intimidation — to show WHY action is needed. It’s evidence of urgency, not contradiction.
Question 3
What perspective is MISSING from this historical passage that would make it more complete?
  • AMore information about what Eleanor Roosevelt did at the UN.
  • BThe perspectives of NAACP members and African Americans about whether Truman’s proposals were sufficient.
  • CA list of all the legislation Truman signed as president.
  • DA comparison between Truman’s and Roosevelt’s civil rights records.
Correct: B — Historical texts often center the perspective of powerful figures. A more complete account would include how those most affected experienced these proposals.
Question 4
Truman invokes Lincoln’s words at the end. How does this use of historical reference function rhetorically?
  • AIt shows that Lincoln would have opposed civil rights legislation.
  • BIt proves that the civil rights movement began during the Civil War.
  • CIt aligns Truman’s cause with a revered figure and frames it as part of a long American tradition.
  • DIt suggests that Truman’s specific policies mirror Lincoln’s.
Correct: C — Invoking Lincoln borrows moral authority and frames Truman’s cause as continuous with America’s highest ideals.
★ GED TEST TIP: Historical texts were written at a specific time, by a specific person, for a specific audience. Always ask: whose perspective is centered? Whose is absent?
